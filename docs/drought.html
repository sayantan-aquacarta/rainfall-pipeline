How to Read SPI Values · Layman's Guide Standardised Precipitation Index · McKee et al., 1993

The Standardised Precipitation Index (SPI) measures how much rainfall a region received compared to its long-term historical average, expressed as a single number on a common scale. SPI = 0 means exactly average; positive numbers mean more rain than usual; negative numbers mean less. Each unit of SPI roughly equals one standard deviation — a statistician's way of saying "how far from normal."

0
Near Normal
Rainfall is close to the long-term average for this period
−2.0
Exceptional Drought
Rainfall lower than ~97.7% of all historical years — a rare crisis
+2.0
Extremely Wet
Rainfall higher than ~97.7% of all historical years — a rare deluge
Code SPI Range Category What it means on the ground
W2 ≥ 1.3 Severely Wet Far more rainfall than historical average. Risk of flooding, waterlogging, and infrastructure stress. Values well above +2.0 (e.g. SPI ≈ +4) indicate a once-in-decades deluge — the season's rain total is statistically extraordinary.
W1 0.8 to 1.3 Moderately Wet Noticeably above-average rainfall. Reservoirs fill faster, groundwater recharges well. Generally good for agriculture.
W0 0.5 to 0.8 Abnormally Wet Slightly more rain than usual. Minimal risk but worth monitoring if combined with already saturated soils.
NN −0.5 to 0.5 Near Normal Rainfall is within the normal range. No drought or flood concern from precipitation alone.
D0 −0.8 to −0.5 Abnormally Dry Slightly below normal. A watch condition — not yet drought, but crops and pastures may show early stress if this persists.
D1 −1.3 to −0.8 Moderate Drought Noticeable rainfall deficit. Crop stress increases, particularly for rain-fed agriculture. Water supply concerns begin. Irrigation demand rises.
D2 −1.6 to −1.3 Severe Drought Significant deficit. Crop losses likely for non-irrigated fields. Reservoir and groundwater levels drop. Water rationing may be introduced in vulnerable areas.
D3 −2.0 to −1.6 Extreme Drought Major deficit — rarer than 1-in-20 years statistically. Widespread crop failure, severe strain on drinking water, livestock losses likely. Emergency response typically warranted.
D4 < −2.0 Exceptional Drought Historically extreme deficit. Less than 2.3% of years see this level. Severe food and water crisis, mass crop failure, and long-term groundwater depletion. Comparable to historic droughts like 1972 or 2002 at a subdivision level.
Reading the time-series chart: When you select a subdivision above, the SPI line chart shows how conditions evolved through the season. A line rising steeply above +1.3 signals a wet spell building; a line falling below −0.8 signals the onset of drought stress. The zero line (dashed) is your reference — distance from it tells you severity, direction tells you whether it is wet or dry.

Why values beyond ±3 appear: SPI is computed from a statistical distribution fitted to historical data. Exceptionally rare events — like a subdivision receiving 180% of its normal rain in a season — can produce SPI values of +3 or higher. These are not errors; they indicate a genuinely extraordinary year that lies far outside the historical envelope.
Methodology: SPI computed using a parameterised gamma distribution (shape α = 1/CV² ≈ 9.77; scale β = LPA/α; CV = 0.32 from Parthasarathy et al., 1987). The LPA baseline comes from the IMD "normal" column in the daily rainfall PDF. As the archive grows to ≥ 10 same-season-day observations, the gamma is re-fitted from observed data. Reference: WMO-No. 1090 (McKee et al., 1993). Map shows state/UT boundaries; each region is colored by the average SPI across its IMD meteorological subdivision(s).
